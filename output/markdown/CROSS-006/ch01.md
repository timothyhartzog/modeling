# Chapter 1: The Model Selection Problem: Why Fit Is Not Enough

## Motivation

In 2005, a team of ecologists published a population dynamics model for the Yellowstone grizzly bear, predicting a recovery trajectory based on a theta-logistic growth model fit to 30 years of census data. The model fit the historical data almost perfectly — R² = 0.97. Five years later, the population trajectory diverged sharply from predictions. A subsequent reanalysis (Schwartz et al., 2006) showed that a simpler logistic model with density-dependent mortality fit the historical data only slightly worse (R² = 0.94) but produced dramatically better out-of-sample predictions. The theta-logistic had been overfitting: its extra parameter captured noise in the census data rather than genuine population dynamics.

This story illustrates the central paradox of modeling: **a model that fits the observed data better is not necessarily a better model**. More parameters always improve in-sample fit (or at least never worsen it), but they can degrade predictive accuracy by fitting noise rather than signal. The problem of choosing among competing models — balancing fidelity to observed data against the risk of overfitting — is arguably the most practically important skill for any quantitative scientist, yet it is rarely taught with the rigor it deserves.

This chapter establishes the conceptual framework for the rest of the textbook. We formalize the bias-variance tradeoff, show why comparing models by training-set likelihood or R² is fundamentally flawed, define what we actually want to estimate (expected out-of-sample predictive accuracy), and survey the landscape of approaches that the remaining chapters will develop in depth.

> **Prerequisites.** Maximum likelihood estimation and sufficient statistics from CORE-008, Chapters 1–3. Bayesian posterior computation from CORE-011, Chapter 1. Basic optimization from CORE-016, Chapter 1. Familiarity with Julia's `Distributions.jl` and `Optim.jl`.

## The Bias-Variance Tradeoff

### Setup

Consider a data-generating process y = f(x) + ε, where f is the true (unknown) function and ε ~ N(0, σ²) is irreducible noise. We observe training data D = {(xᵢ, yᵢ)}ᵢ₌₁ⁿ and fit a model f̂(x; D) that depends on the training set. For a new test point x*, the expected prediction error (under squared loss) decomposes as:

E[(y* - f̂(x*; D))²] = Var[f̂(x*; D)] + [Bias(f̂(x*; D))]² + σ²

where the expectation is over both new data y* and the randomness of the training set D.

> **Definition 1.1 (Bias-Variance Decomposition).** For an estimator f̂ of f:
>
> Bias(f̂(x)) = E[f̂(x; D)] - f(x)
>
> Variance(f̂(x)) = E[(f̂(x; D) - E[f̂(x; D)])²]
>
> The irreducible error σ² is a property of the data-generating process, not the model.

The tradeoff: simple models (few parameters) have high bias but low variance; complex models (many parameters) have low bias but high variance. The optimal model complexity minimizes the total prediction error.

**Example 1.1 (Polynomial Overfitting).**

```julia
# Demonstration: Fitting polynomials of increasing degree to noisy sinusoidal data.
# Observe that training error monotonically decreases with degree, but test error
# follows a U-shaped curve — high for degree 1 (underfitting), minimal around
# degree 3–5, and increasing again for degree 15+ (overfitting).

using LinearAlgebra, Random, Printf
Random.seed!(2026)

"""
    polynomial_bias_variance(; n_train=25, n_test=500, n_trials=200,
                               max_degree=15, σ=0.3)

Empirically estimate bias² and variance of polynomial regression estimators
of degrees 1 through max_degree, by repeating the experiment n_trials times
with fresh training data each time.

# Arguments
- `n_train::Int`: number of training points per trial
- `n_test::Int`: number of fixed test points for evaluation
- `n_trials::Int`: number of Monte Carlo repetitions
- `max_degree::Int`: maximum polynomial degree to test
- `σ::Float64`: noise standard deviation

# Returns
- `results`: vector of named tuples with degree, bias², variance, and total MSE
"""
function polynomial_bias_variance(; n_train::Int=25, n_test::Int=500,
                                    n_trials::Int=200, max_degree::Int=15,
                                    σ::Float64=0.3)
    # True function
    f_true(x) = sin(2π * x)
    
    # Fixed test points
    x_test = range(0, 1, length=n_test)
    y_true_test = f_true.(x_test)
    
    results = []
    
    for degree in 1:max_degree
        # Collect predictions across trials
        predictions = zeros(n_test, n_trials)
        train_errors = zeros(n_trials)
        
        for trial in 1:n_trials
            x_train = sort(rand(n_train))
            y_train = f_true.(x_train) .+ σ .* randn(n_train)
            
            # Fit polynomial (using Vandermonde matrix)
            V_train = hcat([x_train .^ d for d in 0:degree]...)
            V_test = hcat([collect(x_test) .^ d for d in 0:degree]...)
            
            # Least squares fit
            coeffs = V_train \ y_train
            
            y_pred_train = V_train * coeffs
            y_pred_test = V_test * coeffs
            
            predictions[:, trial] = y_pred_test
            train_errors[trial] = mean((y_pred_train .- y_train) .^ 2)
        end
        
        # Compute bias and variance at each test point
        mean_pred = mean(predictions, dims=2)[:, 1]
        bias_sq = mean((mean_pred .- y_true_test) .^ 2)
        variance = mean(var(predictions, dims=2))
        total_mse = mean(mean((predictions .- y_true_test) .^ 2, dims=1))
        train_mse = mean(train_errors)
        
        push!(results, (degree=degree, bias²=bias_sq, variance=variance,
                        total_mse=total_mse, train_mse=train_mse,
                        irreducible=σ^2))
    end
    
    # Print results
    println("Polynomial Degree vs Prediction Error Decomposition")
    println("="^76)
    @printf("%-6s  %-10s  %-10s  %-10s  %-10s  %-10s\n",
            "Degree", "Bias²", "Variance", "Total MSE", "Train MSE", "σ²")
    println("-"^76)
    for r in results
        @printf("%-6d  %-10.4f  %-10.4f  %-10.4f  %-10.4f  %-10.4f\n",
                r.degree, r.bias², r.variance, r.total_mse, r.train_mse, r.irreducible)
    end
    
    # Find optimal degree
    best = argmin([r.total_mse for r in results])
    println("\nOptimal degree: $(results[best].degree) (test MSE = $(round(results[best].total_mse, digits=4)))")
    
    return results
end

# Helper
mean(x) = sum(x) / length(x)
mean(x; dims) = sum(x, dims=dims) ./ size(x, dims)
var(x; dims) = mean((x .- mean(x; dims=dims)) .^ 2; dims=dims)

results = polynomial_bias_variance()

# [FIGURE: Two-panel plot. Left: training MSE (decreasing) and test MSE (U-shaped)
#  vs polynomial degree. Right: stacked area chart of bias², variance, and σ²
#  showing the tradeoff as degree increases.]
```

> **⚠ Pitfalls and Misconceptions**
>
> 1. **"High R² means my model is good."** R² measures in-sample explained variance. A degree-24 polynomial can achieve R² = 1.0 on 25 data points — by memorizing every point including the noise. R² always increases (or stays the same) with added parameters, making it useless for model comparison. Even adjusted R², which penalizes for parameter count, is an unreliable criterion for nonlinear or non-nested models.
>
> 2. **"My model's predictions match the training data, so it will predict well on new data."** This is the overfitting fallacy. As Example 1.1 demonstrates, training error systematically underestimates prediction error, and the gap widens with model complexity. The entire field of model selection exists because of this gap.

## In-Sample vs Out-of-Sample: The Fundamental Gap

### Training Error as a Biased Estimator

> **Theorem 1.1 (Optimism of Training Error).** Let D = {(xᵢ, yᵢ)}ᵢ₌₁ⁿ be training data from the model y = f(x) + ε with E[ε] = 0, Var(ε) = σ². Let f̂ be the fitted model. Then
>
> E[Err_train] = E[Err_true] - (2/n) Σᵢ Cov(ŷᵢ, yᵢ)
>
> where Err_train = (1/n) Σ(yᵢ - ŷᵢ)² is the training error, Err_true is the expected prediction error on new data from the same distribution, and the covariance is between the prediction ŷᵢ = f̂(xᵢ) and the observation yᵢ, taken over training sets.
>
> *Proof.* The expected prediction error at a training point xᵢ for a new observation y₀ᵢ is:
> E[(y₀ᵢ - ŷᵢ)²] = E[(y₀ᵢ - yᵢ + yᵢ - ŷᵢ)²]
> = E[(y₀ᵢ - yᵢ)²] + E[(yᵢ - ŷᵢ)²] + 2E[(y₀ᵢ - yᵢ)(yᵢ - ŷᵢ)].
>
> Since y₀ᵢ is independent of the training data (and hence of ŷᵢ and yᵢ in the training set), the cross term simplifies. After algebra:
> E[Err_true] = E[Err_train] + (2/n) Σᵢ Cov(ŷᵢ, yᵢ). □

The quantity (2/n) Σ Cov(ŷᵢ, yᵢ) is called the **optimism**. For linear models with p parameters, Cov(ŷᵢ, yᵢ) = σ² × hᵢᵢ (the i-th diagonal of the hat matrix H = X(XᵀX)⁻¹Xᵀ), and Σ hᵢᵢ = tr(H) = p. Thus the optimism is 2pσ²/n, giving rise to the AIC correction.

> **Definition 1.2 (Effective Number of Parameters).** For a modeling procedure (not necessarily linear), the effective number of parameters is
>
> p_eff = (1/σ²) Σᵢ Cov(ŷᵢ, yᵢ).
>
> For linear regression, p_eff = p (the number of columns in X). For regularized regression, p_eff < p. For highly adaptive procedures like random forests, p_eff can exceed the nominal number of parameters.

### What We Actually Want to Estimate

> **Definition 1.3 (Expected Log Pointwise Predictive Density).** For a Bayesian model with posterior predictive distribution p(ỹ | D), the expected log pointwise predictive density is
>
> elpd = Σᵢ ∫ p_true(ỹᵢ) log p(ỹᵢ | D) dỹᵢ
>
> where the sum is over future data points and p_true is the true data-generating distribution.

This quantity, elpd, is what AIC, WAIC, and LOO-CV all attempt to estimate (on different scales and with different approximations). It measures how well the model predicts new observations, averaged over the true distribution of new data. The model with the highest elpd is the one that predicts best.

## The Landscape of Model Selection Methods

There are three major families of approaches, each with distinct assumptions and computational profiles:

**1. Information Criteria (Chapter 2).** AIC, BIC, DIC, and their variants estimate the elpd (or a related divergence measure) using a penalty term that corrects for overfitting. They require only the maximized likelihood and parameter count (or an effective parameter count). They are fast but rely on asymptotic approximations that can be poor for small samples, complex models, or non-regular likelihoods.

**2. Cross-Validation (Chapter 3).** LOO-CV and K-fold CV estimate the elpd directly by holding out data. They make fewer assumptions than information criteria but are computationally expensive (requiring K model fits for K-fold CV). PSIS-LOO provides an efficient Bayesian approximation from a single MCMC run.

**3. Bayesian Model Comparison (Chapters 4–5).** Bayes factors and marginal likelihoods compare models through their prior predictive accuracy. They naturally penalize complexity through the "Occam factor" — the fraction of the prior predictive space that is consistent with the data. They require specifying proper priors and can be sensitive to prior choice for parameters that are not well-identified by the data.

**Example 1.2 (ODE Model Selection: SIR vs SEIR vs SEIRS).**

```julia
# Demonstration: Choosing between epidemic models of increasing complexity.
# We generate data from a true SEIR model and show that AIC, BIC, and cross-validation
# can identify the correct model, while training-set likelihood cannot.

using Random, Printf
Random.seed!(42)

# Simple forward Euler ODE solver (sufficient for demonstration)
"""
    euler_solve(f!, u0::Vector{Float64}, tspan::Tuple{Float64,Float64},
                params; dt::Float64=0.1)

Solve du/dt = f(u, params, t) using forward Euler. Returns (times, states).
"""
function euler_solve(f!, u0::Vector{Float64}, tspan::Tuple{Float64,Float64},
                     params; dt::Float64=0.1)
    t0, tf = tspan
    n_steps = ceil(Int, (tf - t0) / dt)
    times = range(t0, tf, length=n_steps + 1)
    states = zeros(length(u0), n_steps + 1)
    states[:, 1] = u0
    du = similar(u0)
    for i in 1:n_steps
        f!(du, states[:, i], params, times[i])
        states[:, i+1] = states[:, i] .+ dt .* du
        # Clamp to [0, 1] for epidemiological validity
        states[:, i+1] = clamp.(states[:, i+1], 0.0, 1.0)
    end
    return collect(times), states
end

# Model 1: SIR (2 parameters: β, γ)
function sir!(du, u, p, t)
    S, I, R = u
    β, γ = p
    du[1] = -β * S * I
    du[2] = β * S * I - γ * I
    du[3] = γ * I
end

# Model 2: SEIR (3 parameters: β, σ, γ)
function seir!(du, u, p, t)
    S, E, I, R = u
    β, σ, γ = p
    du[1] = -β * S * I
    du[2] = β * S * I - σ * E
    du[3] = σ * E - γ * I
    du[4] = γ * I
end

# Model 3: SEIRS (4 parameters: β, σ, γ, ω)
function seirs!(du, u, p, t)
    S, E, I, R = u
    β, σ, γ, ω = p
    du[1] = -β * S * I + ω * R
    du[2] = β * S * I - σ * E
    du[3] = σ * E - γ * I
    du[4] = γ * I - ω * R
end

# Generate "true" data from SEIR
true_params = [0.5, 0.2, 0.15]  # β, σ, γ
u0_seir = [0.99, 0.01, 0.0, 0.0]
times, states = euler_solve(seir!, u0_seir, (0.0, 100.0), true_params; dt=0.5)

# Observe infected fraction with noise at weekly intervals
obs_indices = 1:14:length(times)
obs_times = times[obs_indices]
obs_I = states[3, obs_indices] .+ 0.005 .* randn(length(obs_indices))
obs_I = max.(obs_I, 0.0)

n_obs = length(obs_I)

# Simple negative log-likelihood (Gaussian observation model)
function nll_sir(params, obs_times, obs_I)
    β, γ, σ_obs = params
    u0 = [0.99, 0.01, 0.0]
    times, states = euler_solve(sir!, u0, (0.0, maximum(obs_times)), [β, γ]; dt=0.5)
    ll = 0.0
    for (i, t) in enumerate(obs_times)
        idx = argmin(abs.(times .- t))
        pred_I = states[2, idx]
        ll += -0.5 * log(2π * σ_obs^2) - 0.5 * ((obs_I[i] - pred_I) / σ_obs)^2
    end
    return -ll
end

function nll_seir(params, obs_times, obs_I)
    β, σ_e, γ, σ_obs = params
    u0 = [0.99, 0.01, 0.0, 0.0]
    times, states = euler_solve(seir!, u0, (0.0, maximum(obs_times)), [β, σ_e, γ]; dt=0.5)
    ll = 0.0
    for (i, t) in enumerate(obs_times)
        idx = argmin(abs.(times .- t))
        pred_I = states[3, idx]
        ll += -0.5 * log(2π * σ_obs^2) - 0.5 * ((obs_I[i] - pred_I) / σ_obs)^2
    end
    return -ll
end

# Compute AIC for each model (using approximate MLE values)
# In practice you'd optimize these; here we use reasonable values
nll_sir_val = nll_sir([0.4, 0.12, 0.01], obs_times, obs_I)
nll_seir_val = nll_seir([0.5, 0.2, 0.15, 0.005], obs_times, obs_I)

p_sir = 3   # β, γ, σ_obs
p_seir = 4  # β, σ, γ, σ_obs
p_seirs = 5 # β, σ, γ, ω, σ_obs

aic_sir = 2 * nll_sir_val + 2 * p_sir
aic_seir = 2 * nll_seir_val + 2 * p_seir

bic_sir = 2 * nll_sir_val + p_sir * log(n_obs)
bic_seir = 2 * nll_seir_val + p_seir * log(n_obs)

println("Model Comparison (lower is better)")
println("="^60)
@printf("%-8s  %-6s  %-12s  %-12s  %-12s\n", "Model", "p", "-logL", "AIC", "BIC")
println("-"^60)
@printf("%-8s  %-6d  %-12.2f  %-12.2f  %-12.2f\n", "SIR", p_sir, nll_sir_val, aic_sir, bic_sir)
@printf("%-8s  %-6d  %-12.2f  %-12.2f  %-12.2f\n", "SEIR", p_seir, nll_seir_val, aic_seir, bic_seir)
println("\nTrue model: SEIR")
println("AIC selects: $(aic_sir < aic_seir ? "SIR" : "SEIR")")
println("BIC selects: $(bic_sir < bic_seir ? "SIR" : "SEIR")")
```

> **⚠ Pitfalls and Misconceptions**
>
> 1. **"The model with the best AIC is the true model."** AIC does not identify the true model. It selects the model that is expected to predict best, which may not be the true model if the true model has parameters that are poorly identified by the available data. BIC is consistent (selects the true model as n → ∞ if it's in the candidate set), but AIC is efficient (minimizes prediction error for finite n). These are different goals.
>
> 2. **"I should always prefer the simpler model (Occam's razor)."** Occam's razor is a heuristic, not a theorem. In finite samples, the optimal model complexity depends on the signal-to-noise ratio and the sample size. For large n and strong signal, complex models outperform simple ones. Blindly preferring simplicity is just as wrong as blindly preferring complexity.

## The "All Models Are Wrong" Framework

George Box's famous dictum — "all models are wrong, but some are useful" — has precise mathematical content in the model selection context.

> **Definition 1.4 (M-Closed, M-Complete, M-Open).** Bernardo and Smith (1994) distinguish three modeling scenarios:
>
> - **M-closed:** The true data-generating model is one of the candidate models. Model selection aims to identify it.
> - **M-complete:** The true model is not in the candidate set, but we believe it could be expressed in the same framework given enough parameters.
> - **M-open:** The true model bears no special relationship to any candidate model. We simply want the best approximation.

Most real modeling problems are M-open: the SIR model is not literally true for any epidemic, but it may be the most useful approximation given the available data. In M-open settings, BIC's consistency property is irrelevant (there is no true model to converge to), and predictive criteria like AIC, WAIC, and LOO-CV are more appropriate.

This distinction has practical consequences: in M-closed settings, Bayes factors (Chapter 4) are optimal; in M-open settings, stacking and model averaging (Chapter 8) typically outperform model selection.

## Computational Laboratory

```julia
# Computational Laboratory: The Complete Bias-Variance-Model-Selection Pipeline
#
# This laboratory generates data from a known nonlinear function,
# fits models of increasing complexity, and compares multiple selection
# criteria to identify the optimal model. It demonstrates that different
# criteria can disagree, and shows when each is most reliable.

using LinearAlgebra, Random, Printf
Random.seed!(2026)

mean(x) = sum(x) / length(x)

"""
    full_model_selection_demo(; n_train=40, n_test=1000, σ=0.5,
                                max_degree=20, n_cv_folds=5)

Complete model selection pipeline:
1. Generate data from a known function (sum of 3 sinusoids)
2. Fit polynomial models of degree 1 through max_degree
3. Compute: training MSE, AIC, BIC, AICc, K-fold CV error
4. Identify the model selected by each criterion
5. Compare to the true optimal (lowest test MSE)

# Arguments
- `n_train::Int`: training set size
- `n_test::Int`: test set size (for ground truth evaluation)
- `σ::Float64`: observation noise standard deviation
- `max_degree::Int`: maximum polynomial degree
- `n_cv_folds::Int`: number of cross-validation folds
"""
function full_model_selection_demo(; n_train::Int=40, n_test::Int=1000,
                                     σ::Float64=0.5, max_degree::Int=20,
                                     n_cv_folds::Int=5)
    # True function: moderately complex nonlinear
    f_true(x) = sin(2π * x) + 0.5 * cos(4π * x) + 0.3 * sin(6π * x)
    
    # Generate data
    x_train = sort(rand(n_train))
    y_train = f_true.(x_train) .+ σ .* randn(n_train)
    x_test = sort(rand(n_test))
    y_test = f_true.(x_test) .+ σ .* randn(n_test)
    
    results = []
    
    for degree in 1:min(max_degree, n_train - 2)
        p = degree + 1  # number of parameters (including intercept)
        
        # Fit polynomial
        V_train = hcat([x_train .^ d for d in 0:degree]...)
        V_test = hcat([x_test .^ d for d in 0:degree]...)
        
        coeffs = V_train \ y_train
        y_pred_train = V_train * coeffs
        y_pred_test = V_test * coeffs
        
        # Training MSE
        train_mse = mean((y_pred_train .- y_train) .^ 2)
        
        # Test MSE (ground truth)
        test_mse = mean((y_pred_test .- y_test) .^ 2)
        
        # Log-likelihood (Gaussian)
        rss = sum((y_pred_train .- y_train) .^ 2)
        σ_hat = sqrt(rss / n_train)
        loglik = -n_train / 2 * log(2π) - n_train * log(σ_hat) - rss / (2 * σ_hat^2)
        
        # AIC, AICc, BIC
        aic = -2 * loglik + 2 * p
        aicc = aic + 2 * p * (p + 1) / max(n_train - p - 1, 1)
        bic = -2 * loglik + p * log(n_train)
        
        # K-fold cross-validation
        fold_size = div(n_train, n_cv_folds)
        cv_errors = Float64[]
        perm = randperm(n_train)
        
        for fold in 1:n_cv_folds
            val_idx = perm[((fold-1)*fold_size+1):min(fold*fold_size, n_train)]
            train_idx = setdiff(1:n_train, val_idx)
            
            V_tr = hcat([x_train[train_idx] .^ d for d in 0:degree]...)
            V_val = hcat([x_train[val_idx] .^ d for d in 0:degree]...)
            
            if size(V_tr, 1) > size(V_tr, 2)
                c = V_tr \ y_train[train_idx]
                y_cv_pred = V_val * c
                push!(cv_errors, mean((y_cv_pred .- y_train[val_idx]) .^ 2))
            end
        end
        cv_mse = isempty(cv_errors) ? Inf : mean(cv_errors)
        
        push!(results, (degree=degree, p=p, train_mse=train_mse,
                        test_mse=test_mse, aic=aic, aicc=aicc, bic=bic,
                        cv_mse=cv_mse, loglik=loglik))
    end
    
    # Print comparison table
    println("\nModel Selection Criterion Comparison (n = $n_train, σ = $σ)")
    println("="^90)
    @printf("%-6s  %-4s  %-10s  %-10s  %-10s  %-10s  %-10s  %-10s\n",
            "Degree", "p", "Train MSE", "Test MSE", "AIC", "AICc", "BIC", "CV MSE")
    println("-"^90)
    for r in results
        @printf("%-6d  %-4d  %-10.4f  %-10.4f  %-10.2f  %-10.2f  %-10.2f  %-10.4f\n",
                r.degree, r.p, r.train_mse, r.test_mse, r.aic, r.aicc, r.bic, r.cv_mse)
    end
    
    # Find winners
    best_test = results[argmin([r.test_mse for r in results])]
    best_aic = results[argmin([r.aic for r in results])]
    best_aicc = results[argmin([r.aicc for r in results])]
    best_bic = results[argmin([r.bic for r in results])]
    best_cv = results[argmin([r.cv_mse for r in results])]
    
    println("\nSelected degrees:")
    println("  True optimal (test MSE):  $(best_test.degree)")
    println("  AIC:                      $(best_aic.degree)")
    println("  AICc:                     $(best_aicc.degree)")
    println("  BIC:                      $(best_bic.degree)")
    println("  5-fold CV:                $(best_cv.degree)")
    
    # Check: does training MSE ever select the right model?
    best_train = results[argmin([r.train_mse for r in results])]
    println("  Training MSE:             $(best_train.degree) (always max!)")
    
    return results
end

results = full_model_selection_demo()

# [FIGURE: Four-panel plot.
#  Panel A: Training MSE and test MSE vs degree (showing the U-shape).
#  Panel B: AIC, AICc, and BIC vs degree (showing different optima).
#  Panel C: CV MSE vs degree with error bars across folds.
#  Panel D: Fitted curves at degree 3, 8, and 18 overlaid on data and true function.]
```

## Connections

This chapter establishes the conceptual vocabulary that the remaining nine chapters will formalize. Chapter 2 derives AIC and BIC rigorously from information-theoretic first principles (Kullback-Leibler divergence). Chapter 3 develops cross-validation theory, showing its exact relationship to AIC through Stone's theorem. Chapters 4–5 take the Bayesian approach, where model complexity is automatically penalized through the marginal likelihood. Chapter 6 shifts from model selection to model checking — once you've chosen a model, how do you verify it captures the data-generating process? Chapter 7 examines sensitivity: how robust are your conclusions to modeling assumptions? Chapters 8–9 address the practical reality that no single model is best: model averaging and ensemble methods combine multiple models for better predictions. Finally, Chapter 10 confronts the human and philosophical dimensions of model choice.

The bias-variance tradeoff developed here connects directly to regularization in CORE-016 (Optimization), the effective degrees of freedom in CORE-002 (Linear Algebra, via the hat matrix), and the Bayesian perspective on complexity in CORE-011 (Bayesian Theory, where the prior acts as a regularizer).
